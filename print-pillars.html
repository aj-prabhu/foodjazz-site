The 7 Pillars
The Food Jazz Philosophy
Seven ideas at the core of Food Jazz.
Stock for Success
A stocked kitchen is half the cooking already done. The spices, oils, sauces, and tools you keep around decide what you reach for at 7pm on a Tuesday. Restocking is its own creative act, shaping what you'll be able to make for the next month.
What's in the pantry shapes what gets made.
Taste the World
Navigate the world with your tongue. Active tasting is the most important habit you can build, whether you're eating out, cooking at home, or cooking alongside someone else. The more flavors you can identify, the more you can recreate from scratch.
You can't adjust what you can't taste.
Use What You Have
Resourcefulness is the foundation of intuitive cooking. The most memorable meals come from working with what's around: leftovers, seasonal produce, the half-onion you forgot about.
The most memorable meals come from what you already have.
You're Having Fun
Food is meant to be shared. Cooking is connection, therapy, self-care, and love all at once. When the act of feeding yourself and the people around you feels good, everything else gets easier.
Cooking gets fun when it's for someone else.
Flavor, Not Procedure
Every dish is a ratio: salt to fat, acid to sweet, heat to cold. Once you know the ratios, you can swap an ingredient or skip a step and still hit the target. Recipes are one solution to a ratio problem. The real skill is solving the problem yourself.
Learn the ratio. Forget the recipe.
Your Opinion Matters
There is no platonic ideal of a dish. No chef whose version is the right version. If you imagined a meal and it landed on the plate the way you pictured it, you cooked well, whether that's a five-course tasting menu or a peanut butter sandwich. That's the only test.
If it tastes the way you pictured it, you nailed it.
Build a Flavor Pyramid
Cooking is composition. A dish needs contrast: something rich next to something bright, something soft next to something crunchy. A meal needs the same: a heavy main wants a sharp side, a rich dish wants something acidic to cut through. You're not just making food, you're arranging tension between flavors and textures.
A good meal finds balance.
