The Idea

What Food Jazz actually is.

Food Jazz is a way of cooking that starts with what you already know. Here's the bet: you've been eating your whole life. You have strong opinions. You can walk into a restaurant and tell within three bites whether the chef is paying attention. You have, by any reasonable definition, taste. The only thing missing is a translation layer between what you know you want to eat and what ends up on your plate. That translation is what we're going to build.

There Are Two Ways To Cook

Most people learn the first way and assume it's the only one. It's not.

Path One
By recipe
You collect dishes you can repeat. You buy ingredients for those specific dishes. If something is missing, you don't cook. If you have an extra hour, you make the same thing slower. It works, but it never compounds — your fortieth dish takes as much effort as your tenth.
Path Two · The Food Jazz Way
By feel
You learn the underlying patterns: what fat does, what acid does, how heat changes texture. You build dishes from those. You walk into a kitchen, look at what's there, and make something. If an ingredient is missing, you swap it. If you have an extra hour, the food gets better, not just longer.

The second way is what every grandmother and every working chef does on a Tuesday night. It isn't talent. It's a different mental model, learned in a different kind of classroom. That's what we're after.

Three Things That Have Been In Your Way
  • You were taught there's a "right" way. That if you mess up the bolognese, you ruined it. That's a grease fire waiting to happen. There is no Platonic stir fry you're supposed to replicate. There's a hot pan, some ingredients, and your own sense of what tastes good. We start there.
  • You learned alone, in fragments. Cookbooks, TikTok videos, a $300 weekend dumpling class. Cooking has been taught in community for most of human history — by a parent, a grandparent, a roommate, a coworker. That part isn't optional. It's how the skill actually transfers.
  • Your kitchen isn't built for you. Most people have a kitchen stocked from forgotten experiments — a half jar of harissa, three different vinegars, a spice rack of one-recipe purchases. We're going to rebuild the whole setup around the food you actually want to eat.

The Plan

The shape of the month.

A pre-week to observe how you actually eat, then four weeks built around heat: what's possible without it, what high heat does, what slow heat does, and how to put it all together. The same handful of ingredients reappears across the month at different temperatures, so you can feel firsthand how the same garlic, the same tomato, the same onion becomes four entirely different ingredients depending on what you do to it. By the end, you're hosting a dinner party.

Pre-Week
Observation
You eat how you normally eat — delivery, restaurants, what you reach for at night. Text me each day. Everything that comes next gets built around your taste, not around recipes. Even the grocery run starts looking the same every week, because you're shopping for what you actually like.
Week 1
Mixtures
Cold food, no stove. Dressings, grain bowls, pickles, a full spice tasting session. We build your palate before anything gets hot.
Week 2
High Heat
Wok, cast iron, stir fry. We go to the grocery store together and I show you how to shop without a recipe list. Things get loud and fast.
Week 3
Low & Slow
Braising, baking, cooking for other people. You learn that patience is a technique, and that leftovers improve overnight.
Week 4
Composition
Full meals, not just dishes. You plan the plate, balance the table, execute it, and feed people. Your most independent week.
After
Dinner Party
You host. I show up as a guest. Your food, your people, no recipes in sight. This is what you've been building toward.
The Daily Rhythm

The weekly sessions are where we cook together and the big lessons land. Between sessions, a small daily loop keeps you in motion.

Morning
A prompt arrives in your texts — specific in Week 1, increasingly open-ended as the month goes on.
Your Day
Cook it. Set a timer when you start so you actually see how long things take. Send me a photo or quick video.
My Response
Specific feedback within a few hours — voice memo or text. Not "looks great." Something you can use.
Weekly
In-person session at your place: we cook together, debrief the week, and I hand off reference materials as we go.

The Mindset

How to show up, and where this leaves you.

The whole month rests on a few small commitments. None of them are heavy on their own. Together they make the difference between a course you took and a habit you keep.

1
Cook every day, even when it's small, even when it's bad. Especially when it's bad. A meal that doesn't work teaches you more than one that does, because you actually have to figure out why.
2
Set a timer when you start cooking. Not to race — to know. Most weeknight meals take under twenty minutes once you stop second-guessing yourself. You'll be surprised.
3
Send me photos. Every day. This is how I track where you are and tailor what comes next. If I don't hear from you, I'll follow up. Consider yourself warned.
4
Be honest. If something tastes bad, say so. If you skipped a day, tell me. If you're confused, ask. There's no judgment here, ever.
5
Let yourself be a beginner. Cooking feels vulnerable — you're making something from scratch and asking someone to eat it. That's real. Confidence comes from doing it anyway, and you're going to do it a lot this month.
A heads-up. Around day twelve or fifteen, this will stop feeling exciting and start feeling like routine. That isn't failure — that's where the real skill-building lives. Most people quit right there. Text me when it hits and we'll talk about it.
Happy little accidents. Some of what you make this month won't taste good. That's not failure, that's data. You learn more from a bad vinaigrette than a good one because you have to figure out what went wrong. I'll probably be eating it with you.
Where this leaves you

By day thirty, the way you think about food has changed. Not because of a magic recipe, but because the gap between what you crave and what ends up on your plate has closed.

  • You open the fridge and see options, not obstacles.
  • You taste mid-cook and know what's missing — too acidic, needs fat, almost there.
  • You shop for the week, not for a recipe you'll make once and forget.
  • You cook for the people you love without asking them to wait while you check your phone.
See you in the kitchen.
